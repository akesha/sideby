
[P]
[P]Teaching, Learning, and AI
[P]
[P]A self-paced workbook drawn from a panel discussion on educator learning about AI
[P]
[P]
[P]The panel
[P]
[P]This workbook is built from a panel discussion on how educators are learning to incorporate AI into their work. The conversation focused on the how, not the what: how educators develop their own AI fluency, how that connects to student learning, and what habits help them stay current in a field that is changing weekly.
[P]
[P]The panelists were:
[P]
[P]
[P]Akesha Horton, PhD, Director of Academic Engagement and Learning, Indiana University Bloomington (Luddy School of Informatics, Computing, and Engineering)
[P]
[P]
[P]Yousuf Marvi, Math Teacher and ELD Coach, Sierra Vista Middle School, Irvine Unified School District
[P]
[P]
[P]Anne Fensie, PhD, Director of the Center for Teaching and Learning, University of Maine at Presque Isle
[P]
[P]
[P]
[P]
[P]How to use this workbook
[P]
[P]Plan for 60 to 75 minutes. You can do it in one sitting or break it into four shorter sessions. You will need a course, program, or assignment you actually work on. Pick something real. Generic answers produce generic results.
[P]
[P]By the end you will have:
[P]
[P]
[P]A short statement of your own why for AI in your teaching practice.
[P]
[P]
[P]A position on six tensions the panel surfaced.
[P]
[P]
[P]A revised assignment, policy, or activity ready to test.
[P]
[P]
[P]A one-page commitment document with a date to revisit it.
[P]
[P]
[P]
[P]
[P]What is inside
[P]
[P]
[P]Section 1: What was said. Curated panel excerpts with reading prompts (about 15 minutes).
[P]
[P]
[P]Section 2: Where you stand. Values mapping on six tensions (about 15 minutes).
[P]
[P]
[P]Section 3: Apply it. A guided revision of one assignment, policy, or activity (about 25 minutes).
[P]
[P]
[P]Section 4: Commit. Your one-page commitment document (about 15 minutes).
[P]
[P]
[P]Back matter. Reading list and a guide to running this as a live workshop with your team.

[P]
[P]A NOTE BEFORE YOU START
[P]
[P]The panelists do not agree on everything. That is the point. This workbook is not designed to convince you that AI is good or bad for teaching. It is designed to help you make a clear, defensible decision in your own context, with your own students, for your own reasons.
[P]
[P]If your honest answer to a prompt is “I don’t know yet,” write that down. It is a real position.

[P]
[P]Exchange 1: Why are you doing this?
[P]
[P]The moderator opened with a deceptively simple question. Each panelist landed somewhere different. None of them led with personal efficiency, which the moderator noticed and named.
[P]
[P]“All technology was designed to make a profit. Even Canvas is based on WordPress, which was not designed for teaching or learning. With AI, I like the agency that it provides to educators, to be able to not worry about the cost involved in creating the types of tools that they need. It puts us, as educators, in the design seat.”
[P]
[P]Akesha Horton, citing Punya Mishra
[P]
[P]“My students are my why. When AI came about, I thought about multimodality. I do not have to wait around for text processing only. How do I understand what my novice language learner is doing in transformations? It completely transformed the way I think about the possibility of every single student.”
[P]
[P]Yousuf Marvi
[P]
[P]“I dug into a lot of learning. My chapter was about how to use AI to help and not hinder learning. I really wanted students to understand: how does learning happen? Where do you need that productive struggle? What can you offload? My why is to figure out the potential, but also where the dangers are.”
[P]
[P]Anne Fensie
[P]
[P]

[P]Write three sentences. What is your why? If your honest answer is efficiency, write that. If it is professional pressure or institutional expectation, write that. The rest of this workbook will work either way. What matters is that the answer is true.

[P]Exchange 2: Friction is where learning happens
[P]
[P]Friction came up early and stayed central. The panel converged on a paradox: AI is engineered to remove friction, but learning depends on the right kind of friction staying in place. The panelists differed on how to handle this.
[P]
[P]“I like to use Daniel Willingham’s quote of memory as the residue of thought. That is what it comes down to. We do not just absorb something and move on. We think about it, and that is what we are remembering.”
[P]
[P]Anne Fensie
[P]
[P]“Whenever the learning really occurs, it is in the failures of AI. When AI fails, that is when you realize you need that human in the loop for expertise. My goal is to make sure we are not erasing that friction when I am helping faculty develop their lessons.”
[P]
[P]Akesha Horton
[P]
[P]“AI is designed not to have friction. That is the whole point of it. The big ask of the educator is how to bring that in. I call it effort over output. If your effort over output ratio is close to one, you have learning happening. As soon as it goes way off from one, you have problems on either side.”
[P]
[P]Yousuf Marvi
[P]

[P]Exchange 3: Triumphs, disasters, and the human in the loop
[P]
[P]When the moderator asked about evolved thinking and personal habits, the panelists got specific. Anne told a story that earned a flinch from the room.
[P]
[P]“I was developing a platform to track all of our online course content. I had a conversation with AI about a quick update to all of the classes. I just followed the steps. It overrode two thirds of the classes with the same information, completely wiping out two thirds of my database. No undo button. No backup. I spent ten hours in my hotel room at AAC&U manually copying and pasting changes back. I am now much more critical of AI output. I am afraid of getting my humanness out.”
[P]
[P]Anne Fensie
[P]
[P]“In the past two months I have made about thirty different apps. Are they good? I do not know. They are in different ranges of quality. But I am learning how. I am meeting people through peer networks who I can ask questions about which tools to use, what I am giving up in terms of privacy. It is not just project-based learning, it is peer-based learning, and it is not being intimidated to ask questions.”
[P]
[P]Akesha Horton
[P]
[P]“We have an AI Pioneers PLC where teachers are paid to opt in and experiment. There is a teacher on special assignment whose job is to lead and facilitate it. I have been fortunate to be in a district that has thought about what structured peer collaboration looks like. So we are constantly learning from each other.”
[P]
[P]Yousuf Marvi

[P]Section 3: Apply it
[P]
[P]Pick one of three pathways. Each takes about 25 minutes. Pick the one that maps to a real problem you have right now, not the one that sounds most interesting in the abstract. You can come back and do the others later.

[P]
[P]
[P]
[P]Pathway A is for assignment redesign. Pick this if you have an assignment that AI is changing whether you like it or not.
[P]
[P]
[P]Pathway B is for course-level policy. Pick this if your students do not know what is and is not allowed in your course, or if your current policy is a copy-paste.

[P]
[P]Pathway A: Redesign an assignment to preserve productive struggle
[P]
[P]Take one assignment from the course you actually teach. Walk it through these prompts in order.
[P]
[P]WORKED EXAMPLE
[P]
[P]An undergraduate writing course assigns a 1,500-word analysis of a primary source. Students used to spend the first week annotating. AI flattens the annotation step in seconds. The instructor preserved friction by changing the deliverable: students now submit the annotation log itself, with timestamps, alongside the analysis. The struggle moved from “find the moves” to “notice your own thinking,” which is closer to what the assignment was really teaching.

[P]Pathway B: Draft a course-level AI policy that names the why
[P]
[P]Most AI policies are a yes or no. The panel’s framing suggests something more useful: a policy that tells students why your course makes the choices it does. It is shorter than you think.
[P]
[P]WORKED EXAMPLE
[P]
[P]A graduate research methods course allows AI for literature search and brainstorming, requires disclosure for any text used in drafts, and prohibits AI for the final synthesis paper. The policy explains in two sentences that the course is teaching students to develop a defensible argument from messy evidence, that AI is excellent at the messy evidence step and unreliable at the defensible argument step, and that the course design follows that distinction.

[P]Pathway C: Define a problem of practice and identify whether AI is part of the solution
[P]
[P]Yousuf’s closing nugget: AI should be part of the process, not the starting and ending point of the conversation. This pathway asks you to start where he says to start.
[P]
[P]WORKED EXAMPLE
[P]
[P]A faculty developer noticed that adjunct instructors in one program were spending hours per week writing detailed feedback on early drafts and burning out. The problem of practice was not “we need an AI feedback tool.” It was “adjunct workload is unsustainable, and feedback quality is uneven across sections.” Once named, AI showed up as one possible piece (a feedback rubric assistant for instructors) alongside non-AI options (a peer review structure, a reduction in feedback frequency). The decision to use AI followed the problem instead of leading it.

[P]Reading list
[P]
[P]Each of these came up in the panel, either by name or by clear reference. Start with whichever sits closest to your six marks in Section 2.
[P]
[P]
[P]
[P]
[P]On how learning works
[P]
[P]Daniel Willingham, Why Don’t Students Like School? A Cognitive Scientist Answers Questions About How the Mind Works and What It Means for the Classroom. The “memory is the residue of thought” claim Anne quoted comes from this book.
[P]
[P]
[P]
[P]
[P]On educators as designers, not consumers, of technology
[P]
[P]Punya Mishra and Matthew Koehler’s work on TPACK (Technological Pedagogical Content Knowledge) is the foundation Akesha referenced. Mishra’s argument that there is no such thing as educational technology, because all technology was designed for profit, is a useful frame for evaluating any tool you adopt.
[P]
[P]
[P]
[P]
[P]On the sociotechnical lens
[P]
[P]Safiya Umoja Noble, Algorithms of Oppression: How Search Engines Reinforce Racism (NYU Press, 2018).
[P]
[P]Ruha Benjamin, Race After Technology: Abolitionist Tools for the New Jim Code (Polity, 2019), and Viral Justice: How We Grow the World We Want (Princeton University Press, 2022). Akesha’s working principle (someone benefits from a technology, and someone else is usually being disenfranchised by the same one) is a one-line summary of Benjamin’s argument.
[P]
[P]
[P]
[P]
[P]On the environmental cost of AI
[P]
[P]Anne referenced the energy comparison tool what-uses-more.com, which lets you compare the energy use of an AI task against a familiar baseline like watching Netflix. Pair it with Hannah Ritchie’s public data work on energy and Sasha Luccioni’s research on the environmental costs of generative AI for a fuller picture.
[P]
[P]
[P]
[P]
[P]On educator self-directed learning
[P]
[P]Indiana University’s Generative AI course is open to anyone. Search for “GenAI IU” or contact Akesha for the current URL. For peer-based learning structures, look up your local equivalents of Irvine Unified’s AI Pioneers PLC: paid, opt-in, structured time for educators to experiment together with a facilitator.

[P]Format 2: A four-week department series
[P]
[P]Best for a CTL or program with a regular meeting cadence.
[P]
[P]
[P]Week 1: Section 1 plus a shared reading from the reading list. Discussion-based.
[P]
[P]
[P]Week 2: Section 2 as a live values-mapping activity. Surface where the department actually disagrees.
[P]
[P]
[P]Week 3: Section 3. Each person brings one assignment, policy, or problem of practice. Workshop in pairs.
[P]
[P]
[P]Week 4: Section 4 plus a peer-accountability structure. Pair people up; they meet again in 60 days to compare what actually changed.

[P]PRE-WORK AND FOLLOW-UP SUGGESTIONS
[P]
[P]Pre-work: ask participants to bring one assignment and the prior 24 hours of their own AI use written down without judgment.
[P]
[P]Follow-up: schedule a 30-minute check-in 60 days out. Ask each participant which of their three commitments held, which slipped, and what they learned from the slips. The slips are the data.
